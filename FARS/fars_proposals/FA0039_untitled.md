# Prefix-Ratio GRPO for High-Staleness Rollout Replay (ECHO-2 Follow-up)

## Scope and Constraints

- **Paper Type**: Short paper
- **Target Venues**: NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, ACL, EMNLP (or similar top AI conferences)

## Introduction

### Context and Motivation

Reinforcement learning (RL) post-training is increasingly used to improve large language models (LLMs) on tasks with objective feedback, such as competition math or unit-tested code generation. In this setting, each model output can be scored by an automatic verifier (e.g., exact-match on a final numeric answer), and the model is updated to increase the probability of higher-reward outputs. This is often called **reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR)**.

A practical bottleneck in RLVR is that rollout generation (sampling many long responses per prompt) is typically much more expensive than the learner update. Recent systems therefore decouple rollout generation from learning, often by running rollouts on separate machines and streaming trajectories into a replay buffer. This decoupling introduces **policy staleness**: some trajectories were generated by an older policy snapshot than the learner's current parameters. Moderate staleness can improve throughput and reduce cost by hiding communication and straggler delays, but too much staleness can destabilize RL.

ECHO-2 is a recent distributed RL framework that achieves 33–36% cost reduction by centralizing learning on data-center GPUs while distributing rollout generation to cheaper workers over wide-area networks. It makes **bounded staleness** a first-class system knob: each trajectory is tagged by the policy snapshot version that generated it, and the learner only consumes trajectories whose version lag is at most **S learner steps** (maximum policy lag) [ECHO-2](./references/ECHO-2-A-Large-Scale-Distributed-Rollout-Framework-for-Cost-Efficient-Reinforcement-Learning/meta/meta_info.txt). However, ECHO-2 also reports a practical stability boundary: for GRPO training, S≤6 is stable but S=11 “leads to divergence” (Figure 3b; “RL Quality under Bounded Staleness”).

### The Problem

ECHO-2's result suggests that system-level bounded-staleness execution can reduce cost only up to the point where the underlying RL algorithm can tolerate off-policy data. ECHO-2's learner uses a standard token-level likelihood ratio ρ_{t}=π_θ(y_t|x,y_{<t})/π_{\theta_{old}}(y_t|x,y_{<t}) inside a PPO/GRPO-style clipped surrogate objective with KL regularization (Appendix D.3), and it additionally mentions an outer truncated importance ratio for replay-buffer reuse (constant C) [ECHO-2](./references/ECHO-2-A-Large-Scale-Distributed-Rollout-Framework-for-Cost-Efficient-Reinforcement-Learning/meta/meta_info.txt).

Recent work argues that token-level importance ratios are a poor approximation in off-policy LLM RL because the *theoretically correct* correction term is a **prefix importance ratio** (a cumulative product over earlier tokens), and that ignoring prefix drift causes training instability when policy lag and sequence length are large. MinPRO (Minimum Prefix Ratio Policy Optimization) proposes a numerically stable surrogate that multiplies each token ratio by the **minimum prior token ratio** along the trajectory, and shows improved stability/performance on math RLVR benchmarks under severe off-policy drift [MinPRO](./references/A-Step-Back-Prefix-Importance-Ratio-Stabilizes-Policy-Optimization/meta/meta_info.txt). Separately, M2PO (Second-Moment Policy Optimization) shows that stale data can remain informative at extreme lag if the trust region suppresses only rare extreme-ratio outliers (via a second-moment constraint) rather than broadly shrinking all updates [M2PO](./references/Prosperity-before-Collapse-How-Far-Can-Off-Policy-RL-Reach-with-Stale-Data-on-LLMs/meta/meta_info.txt).

**Why this is not a trivial re-run of MinPRO**: MinPRO's "off-policy" setup induces staleness by delaying rollout batches by **n global steps** in a mostly synchronous VeRL loop; with 16 optimizer updates per global step, their n=2 setting corresponds to **32–47 parameter updates** of lag between behavior and current policy. ECHO-2's bounded staleness instead arises from a **versioned, asynchronous rollout plane**: trajectories in the replay buffer come from a *mixture* of snapshot versions v, and the learner only enforces an admissibility constraint v ≥ v_t − S (no fixed delay). Moreover, ECHO-2's staleness is measured in **learner steps** (each step includes two model updates), and its learner objective uses both KL regularization and an outer truncated importance ratio (constant C) for replay reuse (Appendix D.3). Because the staleness mechanism and objective differ, it is unclear a priori whether MinPRO-style prefix weighting transfers to the ECHO-2 regime that empirically diverges at S=11.

This motivates a concrete question at the boundary between RL algorithms and distributed systems:

**When bounded-staleness systems (like ECHO-2) become unstable at higher staleness budgets, is the failure primarily due to the token-level ratio approximation, and can a prefix-aware ratio surrogate stabilize training better than simply tightening the token-level clipping range?**

A positive answer would be actionable for practitioners: it would enable using larger S (slower snapshot publication, cheaper and more heterogeneous rollout workers) without collapse, improving the cost-quality frontier of distributed RLVR.

### Key Insight and Hypothesis

**Key insight**: In long-sequence RLVR, off-policy drift compounds along a trajectory. Even if most token ratios ρ_t are near 1, an “unlikely prefix under the current policy” can make later gradients unreliable; token-level clipping does not explicitly detect this prefix-level drift. This effect should worsen as staleness increases because the behavior policy diverges farther from the learner.

**Hypothesis**: Replacing the token-level ratio in GRPO with a **prefix-aware importance-ratio surrogate** (MinPRO-style minimum prefix ratio) will stabilize training in the high-staleness regime where vanilla GRPO diverges in ECHO-2, and will achieve better final RL quality than a strong baseline that simply tightens token-level clipping.

The most likely alternative explanation is that high-staleness instability is not specifically due to prefix drift, but due to generic excessive update magnitude (e.g., clip range too wide). Our experiment includes a tighter-clipping baseline to distinguish “prefix matters” from “any extra regularization suffices.”

## Proposed Approach

### Overview

We propose **Prefix-Ratio GRPO**: a drop-in modification to GRPO/PPO-style RLVR that replaces the per-token importance ratio with a prefix-aware surrogate inspired by MinPRO, while keeping the rest of the learner objective (advantage normalization, KL regularization, replay-buffer bounded staleness) unchanged.

The method is designed to be **learner-side only**: it does not require new rollouts, new reward computation, or changes to the distributed rollout plane. It only requires access to behavior-policy token log-probabilities for stored trajectories (commonly logged in RLHF/RLVR pipelines).

### Method Details

#### Setting

Each rollout is a prompt-response trajectory (x, y) with scalar reward r, generated by a behavior/sampler policy π_{beh} that may lag behind the learner policy π_θ by up to S learner update steps (bounded staleness). Following GRPO, rewards are normalized within rollout groups to obtain token-level advantages Â_{t}.

#### Baseline GRPO objective (token-level)

ECHO-2 describes a GRPO-style clipped surrogate with KL regularization that uses a token likelihood ratio

ρ_t(θ) = exp( log π_θ(y_t|x,y_{<t}) − log π_{beh}(y_t|x,y_{<t}) ),

and then applies clipping ρ_t ∈ [1−ε_c, 1+ε_c] (Appendix D.3) [ECHO-2](./references/ECHO-2-A-Large-Scale-Distributed-Rollout-Framework-for-Cost-Efficient-Reinforcement-Learning/meta/meta_info.txt).

#### Prefix-Ratio GRPO (ours)

We compute token log-ratios

ℓ_t = log π_θ(y_t|x,y_{<t}) − log π_{beh}(y_t|x,y_{<t}).

Let the token ratio be ρ_t = exp(ℓ_t). Following MinPRO, define the **minimum prefix token ratio** before position t:

\underline{ρ}_t = min_{k < t} ρ_k = exp( min_{k < t} ℓ_k ).

We then replace the token ratio ρ_t with a prefix-aware surrogate

\tilde{ρ}_t = \underline{ρ}_t · ρ_t.

Intuition: if *any earlier step* already became unlikely under the learner (a small minimum token ratio), then later-token gradients are downweighted even if the local token ratio ρ_t is not extreme.

We use \tilde{ρ}_t inside the same clipped surrogate form (clipping and KL regularization) to keep the modification minimal.

#### Strong baseline for mechanism isolation: tighter token-level clipping

To test whether the prefix structure is necessary, we include a baseline that keeps the token-level ratio ρ_t but reduces ε_c (e.g., from 0.2 to 0.1). This directly increases the trust-region strength on the same axis as our method, without introducing unrelated changes (e.g., learning rate tuning).

### Key Innovations

- **System-meets-algorithm diagnosis**: Targets a concrete failure mode reported by a distributed RL system (ECHO-2's GRPO divergence at S=11) and tests whether the underlying cause is the token-level ratio approximation rather than staleness itself.
- **Minimal learner-side fix**: A drop-in ratio replacement compatible with common RLVR implementations (GRPO/PPO-style) and bounded-staleness replay buffers.
- **Decisive 3-condition test**: Separates a prefix-specific mechanism claim from generic stabilization by including a stronger token-level clipping baseline.

## Related Work

### Field Overview

Modern RLHF/RLVR training of LLMs is increasingly shaped by systems constraints: rollouts dominate compute and are often executed on separate inference workers, motivating asynchronous or bounded-staleness training pipelines. This setting blends (i) system-level mechanisms (rollout–learner decoupling, snapshot dissemination, staleness control) with (ii) algorithm-level corrections (importance sampling, trust regions, clipping/masking, and reward/advantage normalization). In long-sequence LLM RL, off-policy corrections are particularly delicate because importance ratios can grow or shrink exponentially with sequence length, and because reward signals are often sequence-level.

Recent work has explored both sides: distributed/asynchronous RLHF systems that manage staleness (e.g., ECHO-2, AReaL, StreamRL), and algorithmic methods that stabilize off-policy LLM RL under lag (e.g., MinPRO, M2PO, GSPO, CISPO, trust-region masking). Our proposal focuses on the intersection: staleness-controlled replay as in ECHO-2, but with a prefix-aware correction inside GRPO.

## Experiments

### Experimental Setup

**Goal**: A minimal reproduction of “high staleness destabilizes GRPO” and a decisive test of whether prefix-aware correction helps beyond tighter token-level clipping.

**Base Models:**

| Model | Size | Download Link | Notes |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Qwen3-8B (base) | 8B | https://huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen3-8B | Matches ECHO-2 model family; if unavailable, fall back to Qwen2.5-7B |

**Training Data (RL prompts):**

| Dataset | Purpose | Size | Download Link | License |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| DAPO-Math-17K | RLVR training prompts + verifier answers (primary) | ~17,000 problems | https://huggingface.co/datasets/haizhongzheng/DAPO-Math-17K-cleaned | Check dataset card |
| AIME24 | evaluation-only benchmark | 30 problems | https://huggingface.co/datasets/HuggingFaceH4/aime_2024 | Check dataset card |

Rationale: AIME24 is too small for stable RL training (high variance); we use it only for evaluation, matching ECHO-2's primary metric (avg@64).

**System / codebase:**
- Implement using **veRL** (HybridFlow) GRPO training pipeline: https://github.com/volcengine/verl
- Implement bounded staleness by maintaining a rollout policy snapshot updated every κ steps (default κ=S−1 as in ECHO-2) and tagging trajectories by snapshot version; the learner samples only trajectories with version lag ≤ S.

**Resource Estimate** (must fit ≤768 A100 GPU-hours):
- ECHO-2 reports training time per step on Qwen3-8B of ~1631–1649 seconds for ECHO-2 settings and ~1508 seconds for a synchronous baseline (Table 2 text in “Broadcast and Cost-Aware Provisioning”) [ECHO-2](./references/ECHO-2-A-Large-Scale-Distributed-Rollout-Framework-for-Cost-Efficient-Reinforcement-Learning/meta/meta_info.txt).
- Plan: run **60 learner updates** per condition, **3 conditions**, **3 random seeds** (total 9 runs), using **8×A100-80GB** split as 4 GPUs for training + 4 GPUs for rollout generation (co-located if needed).
- **Compute budget note**: Multi-seed RL is expensive. We pre-register a staged schedule that keeps the **decisive test to 3 methods** while fitting ≤768 A100-GPU-hours:
  - **Stage 0 (sanity)**: Vanilla GRPO @ **S=6** for 10 updates (1 seed) to confirm the pipeline is stable in the regime ECHO-2 reports as safe.
  - **Stage A (stability test)**: **12 updates** × **3 methods** × **3 seeds** at **S=11**, with early stopping when a collapse criterion triggers.
  - **Stage B (quality check)**: extend **only stable method(s)** to **60 updates** on **1 seed each** (at most 2 runs: tight-clipping and prefix-ratio) and report final AIME24 avg@64.

Using ECHO-2's co-located baseline step time (T_train≈1508s/step for verl-sync; Table 2) as a conservative upper bound, the worst-case budget is:
- (Stage 0: 10 + Stage A: 12×9 + Stage B: 48×2) = 214 updates
- GPU-hours ≤ 214 × (1508/3600) × 8 ≈ **717 A100-GPU-hours**, leaving margin for overhead.

This preserves a clean 3-condition decisive test for *stability* and a small confirmatory check for *final quality* within budget.

**Infrastructure constraints**: no GUI, no browser, no heavy simulators (satisfied).

### Benchmarks and Metrics

| Benchmark | Description | Metrics | Split | Download Link | Evaluation Script |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| AIME24 | 30 competition math problems with numeric answers | avg@64 accuracy; reward curve | test (all) | https://huggingface.co/datasets/HuggingFaceH4/aime_2024 | simple numeric-answer checker (regex extract final integer; compare to ground truth mod 1000) |

**Stability metrics (automated; pre-registered):**
- **Numerical failure**: any NaN/Inf in loss or gradients.
- **KL blow-up**: mean KL(π_θ || π_ref) over a training batch exceeds **2×** its median value over the first 5 updates, for **≥3 consecutive** learner updates.
- **Reward collapse**: moving-average batch reward over a 5-update window drops below **50%** of its value at update 5, for **≥5 consecutive** learner updates.

We will report which failure mode triggered collapse (numerical vs KL vs reward).

## Success Criteria

**Criterion 1: High-staleness stability**
- Hypothesis: Prefix-Ratio GRPO avoids collapse at high staleness (starting at S=11), where vanilla GRPO collapses or materially degrades.
- Validation: Under the same training budget, vanilla GRPO triggers an automated collapse criterion while Prefix-Ratio GRPO does not.

**Criterion 2: Prefix effect vs generic clipping**
- Hypothesis: Prefix-Ratio GRPO achieves higher final AIME24 avg@64 than the tighter-clipping baseline at the same S and compute budget.
- Validation: Final AIME24 avg@64 of Prefix-Ratio GRPO exceeds tighter-clipping GRPO by **≥5 percentage points absolute** (or, if both are stable but accuracy is noisy, Prefix-Ratio GRPO has ≥0.05 higher mean batch reward over the last 10 updates).

(If tighter clipping ≈ prefix ratio and both >> vanilla, we report this as the practical conclusion that stronger token-level clipping suffices to push staleness higher.)

## Impact Statement

If successful, this work provides a low-friction learner-side change that extends the safe staleness range in bounded-staleness rollout systems. This would let practitioners run RLVR with less frequent policy broadcast and cheaper, more heterogeneous rollout workers, improving the cost-quality frontier of distributed post-training without changing reward computation or rollout infrastructure.
